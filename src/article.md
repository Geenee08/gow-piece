# 8,897 Lines of Wasseypur

*We read every line of the Gangs of Wasseypur scripts — both films, 288 pages, 64 years of story. Here are the patterns hiding inside.*

---

## I. Four Generations, One Trick

The Gangs of Wasseypur saga spans 288 pages, 8,897 lines, and 64 years. It tells the story of four generations of one family. We parsed every line of both scripts to track every character's presence across the story.

Here's what we found: the saga pulls the same structural trick four times. Each generation's successor is nearly invisible while their predecessor is alive — then explodes into dominance after the predecessor dies.

**[VISUALIZATION: Four overlapping area curves — Shahid, Sardar, Faisal, Definite — across the combined 288-page x-axis. Each curve rises as the previous one falls to zero.]**

The numbers:

| Character | Generation | Peak Quarter | Mentions at Peak | First Quarter Mentions |
|---|---|---|---|---|
| Shahid Khan | 1 | P1-Q1 | 110 | 110 |
| Sardar Khan | 2 | P1-Q2 | 168 | 32 |
| Faisal Khan | 3 | P2-Q1 | 99 | 99 |
| Definite | 4 | P2-Q4 | 49 | 4 |

Shahid Khan has 110 mentions in the first quarter of Part 1 — and then disappears entirely. Zero mentions for the rest of the saga. He doesn't fade. He's amputated.

Sardar Khan, the character many consider the saga's protagonist, has just 32 mentions while his father Shahid is alive. That's 29% of Shahid's presence. Then Shahid is shot by Yadav, and Sardar's mentions quintuple to 168.

The script repeats this pattern with increasing aggression. By Part 2, Definite — the youngest brother who ends up pulling the trigger in the final scene — has just 4 mentions in the first quarter. That's 4% of Faisal's presence. A character who is functionally not in the movie. Then, quarter by quarter, he rises: 4, 47, 38, 49.

**[VISUALIZATION: Small multiples — the "suppression ratio" declining from 29% to 4% across generations. A simple bar chart showing each successor's visibility as a percentage of the patriarch's.]**

The saga doesn't just tell a story about succession. It structurally *performs* succession — hiding each heir in the frame while making you look at the king.

## II. The Survivor's Table

Of the six Khan men who drive the saga, only two survive. The data reveals a clean binary that governs who lives and who dies:

**[VISUALIZATION: The table below, styled as a horizontal bar chart with violence lines on one axis and V.O. count on the other. Survivors highlighted.]**

| Character | Mentions | Voiceovers | Violence Lines | Survives? |
|---|---|---|---|---|
| Shahid Khan | 110 | 0 | 4 | No |
| Sardar Khan | 430 | 1 | 13 | No |
| Danish | 188 | 1 | 2 | No |
| Faisal Khan | 678 | 0 | 26 | No |
| **Definite** | **272** | **11** | **19** | **Yes** |
| **Nasir** | **277** | **67** | **0** | **Yes** |

Nasir Khan has 277 mentions, 67 voiceovers, and zero violence lines across 8,897 lines of script. He is the only character who never touches a gun, never fires a shot, never strikes another person. He's also the saga's narrator — and its only survivor who isn't complicit in someone else's death.

In Part 1, Nasir is 92% participant and 8% narrator. He's in the room. He's part of the family. By Part 2, he's 47% narrator. Nearly half his presence is voiceover — spoken from outside the frame.

**[VISUALIZATION: Nasir's mode shift across 8 quarters. Two stacked bars per quarter — dialogue (blue) and V.O. (orange). The orange grows as the blue shrinks.]**

And his narration has a direction: of the times his voiceover appears within 80 lines of a death scene, 15 come *after* the death and only 4 come *before*. Nasir doesn't foreshadow violence. He eulogizes it. The historian who arrives when the blood is dry.

His final scene: Mumbai. 2009. A house next to railway tracks. Mohsina is drying clothes. Nasir picks up a child and delivers the saga's last words:

> *"Wasseypur ko Faizal ke jaane se koi farak nahi pada. Na hi Ramadhir ke marne se. Woh tab bhi Kurukshetra tha aur aaj bhi Kurukshetra hai… aur shayad hamesha rahega."*

The only Khan who can tell you Wasseypur hasn't changed is the one who left it.

## III. 231 vs. 234

Nagma — the matriarch, Sardar's wife, mother of Danish, Faisal, and Nasir — appears 231 times across the saga.

Ramadhir Singh — the puppet master, the coal baron, the political fixer who orchestrates violence across 64 years — appears 234 times.

Almost the same. Three mentions apart. But what they say is opposite.

**[VISUALIZATION: Two pie charts side by side. Nagma's dialogue: 34% questions, 20% commands, 6% grief. Ramadhir's: mostly orders and plot-driving statements. Or a paired horizontal bar showing their dialogue mode breakdown.]**

Nagma's dialogue in Part 2 is 34% questions — *kya? kab? kaise?* — and 20% commands. Shama Parveen, the widow, has zero commands and 21% grief. Mohsina's dialogue is 39% questions. The women ask. The men answer by shooting.

And then there's Durga.

Durga has 62 mentions across the saga and 16 dialogue lines in Part 2. She speaks less than nearly anyone. But she bookends the entire 288-page story with the two most consequential actions any character takes:

**Part 1's final scene:** Durga picks up the phone and says *"Woh nikal gaye hain"* — he's left. Cut to: Sardar Khan is shot at the petrol pump.

**Part 2's final scene:** *"Definite walks up to Durga."* He has just shot Faisal. JP Singh is thanking the cop. Durga is there.

The woman with 16 dialogue lines makes the phone call that starts the revenge cycle and stands behind the man who ends it. 62 mentions. Maximum structural power. Minimum words.

**[VISUALIZATION: A timeline spanning both films. Two dots — Durga's first mention and last mention — bookending the entire saga, with a thin connecting line. Everything in between is the violence her phone call set in motion.]**

## IV. The Bhagalpur Strobe

Starting at line 3,649 of Part 2, the script enters the Bhagalpur Hotel and doesn't leave for 350 lines. This is the saga's climactic set piece. On paper, it reads like a controlled demolition.

72 scenes. Average length: 4.9 lines — one-third the rest of the film's average of 14.4 lines.

The script alternates between Hotel and Street with 63 switches across 72 scenes. Here's the actual intercut pattern:

**[VISUALIZATION: The sequence `OHOOSOHOHOSHSHSHSHSHSHSHSHSHSHSHSSSHSHSHSHSHSSHS HSSHSHSHSHSSHSSHSHSHSSHS` rendered as a horizontal strip of alternating colored blocks — Hotel (red) and Street (blue). The alternation itself IS the visual. It should feel like a heartbeat monitor going haywire.]**

For comparison, the rest of Part 2 averages one scene change every 14.4 lines. The Bhagalpur sequence averages one every 4.9 lines. The script's cutting rhythm *triples* inside this climax.

This acceleration is part of a larger structural pattern across Part 2:

**[VISUALIZATION: Four grouped bars showing scenes-per-quarter in Part 2.]**

| Quarter | Scenes | CUT TO Transitions |
|---|---|---|
| Q1 | 73 | 68 |
| Q2 | 70 | 66 |
| Q3 | 81 | 75 |
| **Q4** | **113** | **114** |

Q4 has 55% more scenes than Q1. The film gets faster as it gets deadlier.

And across the full saga, the acceleration is even more dramatic: Part 1 covers 44 years in 100 scenes (0.8 scenes per page). Part 2 covers what appears to be weeks in 342 scenes (2.0 scenes per page). Part 2 is 2.4 times faster in scene turnover. History moves slowly. Violence moves instantly.

## V. The Constants

Most characters in the saga are waves — they rise and crash. Two are not.

**Ramadhir Singh** has 234 mentions across both films. Here is his presence across all eight quarters:

**[VISUALIZATION: A single flat-ish line chart. Ramadhir's 8-quarter values: 48, 35, 45, 17, 31, 2, 25, 31. Overlaid against the dramatic rise-and-fall curves of the four Khans from Section I. The contrast IS the point.]**

He dips to 2 mentions in P2-Q2 — the story's one moment where you might forget he exists — but he never reaches zero. Four generations of Khans rise and fall against him. He endures. When Faisal finally shoots him in the penultimate scene, Nasir's voiceover tells us it changed nothing.

The second constant isn't a person. It's **daylight**.

76% of all scenes in the saga happen during the day. And deaths follow the pattern: 22 death references occur in exterior scenes, 10 in interiors. The powerful die outdoors, in public, in sunlight. Sardar: petrol pump, day. Faisal: highway, day. The saga's violence isn't hidden. Everyone is watching. No one intervenes.

## The Bookends

The saga's first spoken word belongs to **Sultan** — the enforcer — pointing a gun at a paan shop in 2004.

The saga's last spoken word belongs to **Nasir** — the narrator — holding a child on a rooftop in Mumbai in 2009.

Between those two voices: 288 pages. 442 scenes. 64 years. Four dead Khans. One surviving narrator. And a woman with 16 dialogue lines who, from the first phone call to the last gunshot, was the mechanism that kept the cycle turning.

8,897 lines. We read every one. The saga's last data point was always there, in Nasir's voiceover:

*Nothing changed.*

---

*Data and methodology: Both scripts (GOW Part 1 DUB SCRIPT & GOW Part 2 DUB SCRIPT, for post-production purposes) were parsed line-by-line using Python. Character mentions were counted via exact string matching. Dialogue blocks were identified by screenplay-standard ALL CAPS character sluglines. Violence lines were tagged by proximity of character names to English violence keywords (shoot, fire, gun, kill, stab, pistol). Hindi dialogue was analyzed for agency using Devanagari pattern matching for imperative forms, question markers, and grief/emotional language. Scene headings were identified by INT./EXT. prefixes. All analysis code is available on request.*
